The Spark Within: Exploring the “funda” of Engineering!

Do you remember the joy of discovery as a kid standing near a refrigerator and slowly closing its door to see how the light of the refrigerator automatically switches off? At that time, we didn’t realize it, but we were looking at a problem as an Engineer!

But what happens to these prospective engineers when they “grow up?” Do they disappear? Where are the cool engineers who could build a “chappati maker” for my mother or a dishwasher to save us from the tyranny of “bais?” Where? Why are we not able to find these prospective engineers in the world around us or indeed in ourselves?

The answer to this is that we have not yet completely understood what Engineering is all about! We know that Engineering is about making huge machinery, buildings, roads, and robots. But what we don’t know is the building blocks or atoms of these big miracles! And it's the same in the case of the technologies or methodologies that we use today. We know what that technology is, but we don’t know how to use it to solve real problems faced by you and me!

For instance, we as engineering students of the present era know that the current “Make in India” wave demands engineering in the service of People and Society. Artificial Intelligence along with the Internet of Things is moving the world from technology “out there” in papers and thick books to technology “inside” our lives enabling new possibilities through diminutive slim mobile phones. Machine Learning, the next great engineering discipline, is putting minds and the ability to think and learn inside machines and hence popularising our interaction with machines! The good thing is that we all know a lot superficially about these topics and the bad thing is that we actually don’t know what's “under the hood” so that we may build these wonderful devices ourselves! Leaves us all a bit confused - No?

Our technical section takes us to the roots of wide-ranging technologies widely used by us. Through this newsletter, we want to completely take off the hood covering the magic of technology and help you become the New Age technology wizards that our country and the world needs today. More important is that we wish to explore the idea of “ethical engineering” or our role in making a difference in society through our powers as young engineers. So join us in this exciting adventure of exploring the wizardry of technology.

Regional Innovations Article: Next-Gen Vision Kit

Today, as we speak, more than 350 million people worldwide are partially sighted or blind. A further billion more have, reading difficulties and fatigue. With the loss of vision there come several other obstacles and challenges in our lives. Wouldn’t it be great, if these visually challenged people have a 24x7 assistant with them, to aid them with their daily tasks? With this problem statement in mind, this team of four from Delhi decided to develop the- ‘Next-Gen Vision Kit’, a personal assistant for the visually impaired.

They did not want to develop just a project but wished to create a difference in the lives of the visually impaired, by making them more confident and self-sufficient.
They have implemented a standalone wearable device that can be worn as headgear and will provide the person with all the important features that are needed very often via an integrated button interface and voice commands.

Their project consists of these 5 main features:

  • Object Detection.
  • Face recognition.
  • Navigation and Routing.
  • Text recognition.
  • Walking mode.

In the making, the team was keen on using open-source software to make the product not just affordable but also easy to use and secure. The team, being inspired by the ideology of Atma Nirbhar electronics, is now working on their own single-board computer with enhanced processing power. Vaibhav Singh and team head while talking about this further quotes Alan Kay, while saying that, People who are really serious about software should build their own hardware. The difference between developing a mere project and creating a market-ready product lies in the approach. By addressing a real-world problem of the visually impaired and providing a technological solution for it the team members have shown us the real meaning of being engineers.

e-Yantra recognized these efforts by awarding them the ‘Best Project Implementation’ award in this year’s eYIC. Kudos to this team and their wonderful thought process!

We hope this inspires you, readers, to create something similar of your own and if you or anyone you know already have then, do write to us and we would love to publish such Regional Innovations in this Newsletter!

WHY TO INVEST AS STUDENTS?

Because an early bird catches some real cool worms and you need worms to eat and live and sustain! Really, it’s as simple as that. Warren Buffet (he is a famous guy, Google him if you don’t know. And he’s rich ofcourse) himself started investing at an early age of 19. World is suffering, yes actually suffering, from the ‘problem’ of longevity. “Ayushyaman Bhav” isn’t a blessing anymore. The reason is, you may live long but will you be wealthy and healthy throughout the course of it? Well, it depends on how early and how well you start taking care of these two things. All heroes don’t wear capes and compound interest is one such hero in disguise! Yes, the same topic which we learned in 7th, forgot in 8th and dreaded for most part of it. You can use its compounding powers to invest and reinvest your interest earned on that money money and watch it grow, just like the population does. Compounding is very essential as far as investments are concerned and as the term for the number of years is as raised to the formula, the more the years, the better.

WHERE TO INVEST AS STUDENTS?

Now that we have convinced you to start early, let us also tell you about some low-risk and safe investment options for you all. Consider it as your food preferences and then let’s look at what they mean.

  1. Recurring Deposit (RD): Well, this RD is your good ol’ dal chawal! Nothing fancy, Something you cook regularly, will definitely satiate your basic appetite and will not go wrong! It’s safe guys! On a serious note, RD allows you to make regular monthly deposits and earn interest on investment. It is offered by banks. The minimum amount and interest varies from bank to bank. Minimum amount can be as low as Rs. 100 and interest can be from 5% to 8%. The key drawback is that RD is not tax efficient.
  2. Public Provident Fund (PPF): A person should age like fine wine they say, but PPF is that wine you wanna age with. PPF, just like wine, is better when older and if you hurry you won’t get the taste at all. Moreover, this wine gets you tax exemptions! PPF is a long term investment option since it has a lock-in period of 15 years which can be extended for more blocks of 5 years. It is backed by the Government of India, offering safety and full exemption from Tax. The interest rate is determined by the Central Government, revised periodically. The minimum amount is Rs. 500 and the maximum amount is Rs. 1,50,000 per annum.
  3. Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) in Mutual Funds: Well, this is like an assorted box of chocolates which you can’t buy alone with your pocket-money so a group of people contribute to and buy it. However the chocolates you get are in proportion to the money you chip in. You may not always like the chocolates you get in return so there’s some risk involved. This is essentially what mutual funds are.
    SIP allows you to invest regularly a fixed sum (of your choice) in mutual fund schemes. The fixed amount of money can be as low as Rs. 500. SIP is a mode of investment, the risk factor depends on the mutual fund scheme you choose to invest in, hence it is important to thoroughly study the scheme you plan to invest before putting in any money.
  4. Education and Skills: You need to know what to eat when similarly you need to know when to invest in what and how much. Well, continuing our food analogy this isn’t a piece of cake and what you need is proper knowledge, education and skills. As Benjamin Franklin rightly said, An Investment in Knowledge pays the best interest.

HOW TO INVEST AS STUDENTS:

  1. Invest in a medical insurance irrespective of your age.
  2. If you have to keep a certain amount of finances as back-up keep it in a bank as an RD or FD. The money may not grow much but the principal amount will stay safe and intact.
  3. In case of high risk investments like in those of shares, invest in index funds rather than actively managed funds. ‘Index’ are the top 30 companies of the market and investments there have less chances to go wrong.

So folks, we hope we atleast could give you a direction to your thought process. It’s a continuous process and as said initially there exists no pill for it so start somewhere, keep going and a secure future awaits you all!

Literature Section

BOOK/MOVIE REVIEW:

Akutagawa Ryūnosuke was one of the first prewar Japanese writers to achieve a wide foreign readership, partly because of his technical virtuosity, partly because his work seemed to represent imaginative fiction as opposed to the mundane accounts of the I-novelists of the time, partly because of his brilliant joining of traditional material to a modern sensibility, and partly because of film director Kurosawa Akira's masterful adaptation of two of his short stories for the screen.

“In a Grove” is an early modernist short story by Ryūnosuke Akutagawa which first appeared in the January 1922 edition of Japanese literature monthly “Shincho”. The story starts off as a murder mystery, the murder of a samurai, Takehiro, whose dead body is found in the bamboo groves near Kyoto. The story has three main characters, the samurai Takehiro, the samurai’s wife Masago, and a notorious bandit Tajomaru. The Samurai’s murder is reported by a woodcutter that triggers the investigation. The story is the form of testimonies of people involved. Throughout the story, when the readers were busy inspecting the details to find the murderer, with no proper explanation of the deed, the author subtly answers the question. But should that even be the question? What is the underlying message of the story? Without giving away much we recommend you to devour this classic masterpiece of a short story which is not more than a 20 minutes read and find the answer or rather the correct question yourself!

The biggest, most important beast to come out of Japanese cinema was Rashomon, a 1950 drama set in the samurai era. It’s a 1950 Jidaigeki psychological thriller/crime film directed by Akira Kurosawa, working in close collaboration with cinematographer Kazuo Miyagawa.
Adapted from the story of ‘In a Grove’ with few minor tweaks here and there, the plot of the movie goes back to the 8th century and takes place in Rashomon, the gate of Kyoto, the then imperial city, ruined by warfare. This movie ingeniously enacts the complexities of human nature by exploring the story from different narratives. By adding a narrative of the woodcutter, we get a gist of how everyone wants to be portrayed in a positive light! But now, the question arises, was the woodcutter telling the truth? Kurosawa once said, “Rashomon is a reflection of life and that life does not have a clear meaning!” Its brilliant cinematography and multiple-perspective storytelling are what make this movie truly a masterpiece! Rife with symbolism and rich in action, this movie definitely leaves ‘food for thought’ for the audience.
